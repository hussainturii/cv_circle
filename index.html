Chapter 01The Summer Intern Who Was Going to Solve Vision

It is the summer of 1966. At MIT, a professor named Seymour Papert assigns an undergraduate student a summer project. The goal: connect a camera to a computer and make the machine describe what it sees.

A summer project. That was how long they thought it would take.

Fifty-eight years later, we have spent billions of dollars, built some of the most powerful computers in human history, and employed tens of thousands of researchers — and we are still working on it.

"The problem of computer vision turned out to be one of the hardest problems in all of science."

— Marvin Minsky, MIT AI Lab

That gap — between how easy vision seems and how hard it actually is — is the whole story of this field. Understanding why that gap exists is the first thing you need to know as a CV researcher.

Chapter 02Why Your Brain Is a Miracle You Never Think About

Right now, without any effort, you are doing something extraordinary. You are reading these words, aware of the screen around them, probably sensing the room beyond that, and if someone walked through the door you would recognise their face in under a second.

Your brain takes a flat, 2D image projected onto your retina and reconstructs a full 3D world. It knows which surfaces are closer. It understands that the handle disappearing behind the mug is still a handle. It recognises your friend's face across different angles, lighting, and years.

The core problem

A digital image is just a grid of numbers. Every pixel is three values — red, green, blue — between 0 and 255. That is all a machine sees. No depth. No context. No continuity. From those numbers, it must understand the world. That is Computer Vision.

For a human, seeing is unconscious and instant. For a machine, it requires explicitly solving every sub-problem that evolution spent 500 million years quietly solving for us.

Chapter 03Six Decades in Six Moments

1966
The Summer Project

MIT's "Summer Vision Project" — expected to take weeks. Laid the groundwork for decades of research into edges, shapes, and object boundaries.

1980s
Rules and Hand-Crafting

Researchers wrote explicit rules: "if you see a horizontal line above a vertical one, that's probably a table." It worked barely in controlled settings. The real world broke everything.

1998
LeNet — The First Hint

Yann LeCun's neural network reads handwritten digits on cheques for banks. The idea of learning features from data, rather than hand-coding them, begins to take shape.

2012
AlexNet — The Earthquake

A deep neural network enters the ImageNet competition and wins by a margin so large the entire field pivots overnight. Every modern CV system traces its DNA to this moment.

2017–2020
The Transformer Revolution

Attention mechanisms arrive in vision. Vision Transformers show that the same architecture powering large language models can also read images — often better than anything before it.

Now
Foundation Models

Models like SAM and GPT-4V can see, describe, and reason about images with near-human fluency. The field is being written in real time — and you are joining it now.

Chapter 04The Five Things Machines Learn to See

Computer Vision is not one problem. It is five distinct ways of understanding an image.

The Five Pillars of Computer Vision

🏷️Classification"What is in this image?"The model outputs a label for the whole image. Cat. Car. Traffic sign. The starting point of modern CV.
📦Detection"What is it and where?"Draws bounding boxes around every object and labels each one. The backbone of self-driving and security cameras.
🎨Segmentation"Which pixels belong to what?"Labels every pixel in the image. Powers surgical robots, background removal, and autonomous navigation.
✨Generation"Create an image that never existed."Diffusion models, GANs, AI art. The machine doesn't just understand images — it invents them.
🌐3D Vision"What is the structure of this scene in space?"Depth estimation, point clouds, spatial mapping. How self-driving cars understand distance, how AR anchors objects to physical space, how robots navigate rooms.

Scene 01 — The Street · +3 points

The model analysed this street scene and drew coloured boxes around objects with labels and numbers. Which CV pillar is this — and what do those decimal numbers mean?

Detection. The model identified objects and drew bounding boxes around their exact locations — that separates Detection from Classification, which would just say "this is a street scene" with no positions. The decimals — 0.96, 0.94, 0.89 — are confidence scores. A score of 0.96 means the model is 96% confident it found a car at that location. Setting the right confidence threshold for a given use case is itself an active research problem.

Scene 02 — The Medical Scan · +3 points

A model has analysed a medical scan and coloured different regions, flagging something in red. What task is it performing — and what is it actually doing to the image at the pixel level?

Segmentation. The model assigned every pixel to a region — organ A, organ B, background, or anomaly. This is pixel-level understanding. A bounding box would not work here — a doctor needs the exact boundary of a region, not just roughly where it is. The red dashed region is the model flagging a deviation from what healthy tissue looks like at that pixel distribution — which is exactly where CV in medicine is genuinely useful.

Bonus question · +3 points

In Scene 01, the model detected a person with confidence 0.89 — lower than the cars at 0.96 and 0.94. Give one reason why detecting a person might be harder than detecting a car in a street scene.

Strong answers include: cars have rigid, consistent shapes — a person changes silhouette drastically depending on pose, clothing, and carried objects. Scale variation — a person far away occupies very few pixels. Occlusion — people are often partially hidden. Appearance variation — the model must generalise across thousands of different clothing styles and body types. Any one of these is a valid, research-quality answer.
