Chapter 01The Summer Intern Who Was Going to Solve Vision

It is the summer of 1966. At MIT, a professor named Seymour Papert assigns an undergraduate student a summer project. The goal: connect a camera to a computer and make the machine describe what it sees.

A summer project. That was how long they thought it would take.

Fifty-eight years later, we have spent billions of dollars, built some of the most powerful computers in human history, and employed tens of thousands of researchers — and we are still working on it.

"The problem of computer vision turned out to be one of the hardest problems in all of science."

— Marvin Minsky, MIT AI Lab, 1970s

That gap — between how easy vision seems and how hard it actually is — is the whole story of this field. And understanding why that gap exists is the first thing you need to know as a CV researcher.

Chapter 02Why Your Brain Is a Miracle You Never Think About

Right now, without any effort, you are doing something extraordinary. You are reading these words, aware of the screen around them, probably sensing the room beyond that, and if someone walked through the door you would recognise their face in under a second.

Your brain takes a flat, 2D image projected onto your retina — essentially a grid of light intensities — and reconstructs from it a full 3D world. It knows which surfaces are closer. It understands that the handle disappearing behind the mug is still a handle. It recognises your friend's face even though it looks completely different from a different angle, in different lighting, five years older.

The core problem

A digital image is just a grid of numbers. Every pixel is three values — red, green, blue, between 0 and 255. That's all a machine sees. No depth. No context. No continuity. From those numbers, somehow, it must understand the world. That's Computer Vision.

For a human, seeing is unconscious and instant. For a machine, it requires explicitly solving every sub-problem that evolution spent 500 million years quietly solving for us.

Chapter 03Six Decades in Six Moments

Here is how the field went from a summer project to reshaping the world.

1966
The Summer Project

MIT's "Summer Vision Project" — expected to take weeks. Laid the groundwork for decades of research into edges, shapes, and object boundaries.

1980s
Rules and Hand-Crafting

Researchers wrote explicit rules: "if you see a horizontal line above a vertical one, that's probably a table." It worked — barely — in controlled settings. The real world broke everything.

1998
LeNet — The First Hint

Yann LeCun's neural network reads handwritten digits on cheques for banks. The idea of learning features from data, rather than hand-coding them, begins to take shape.

2012
AlexNet — The Earthquake

A deep neural network enters the ImageNet competition and wins by a margin so large the entire field pivots overnight. Every modern CV system — YOLO, SAM, Stable Diffusion — traces its DNA to this moment.

2017–2020
The Transformer Revolution

Attention mechanisms, originally designed for language, arrive in vision. Vision Transformers (ViT) show that the same architecture powering ChatGPT can also read images — often better than anything before it.

Now
Foundation Models

Models like SAM (Segment Anything) and GPT-4V can see, describe, and reason about images with near-human fluency. The field being written in real time — and you're joining it now.

Chapter 04The Five Things Machines Can Learn to See

Computer Vision is not one problem — it is five distinct ways of understanding an image. Each pillar is its own sub-field, with its own benchmarks, architectures, and open questions.

The Five Pillars of Computer Vision

🏷️ Classification "What is in this image?" The model looks at the whole image and outputs a label. Cat. Car. Traffic sign. The starting point of modern CV.
📦 Detection "What is it, and where?" Draws bounding boxes around every object and labels each one. The backbone of self-driving cars and security cameras.
🎨 Segmentation "Which pixels belong to what?" Labels every single pixel in the image. Powers surgical robots, photo background removal, and autonomous navigation.
✨ Generation "Create an image that never existed." Diffusion models, GANs, deepfakes, AI art. The machine doesn't just understand images — it invents them.
🌐 3D Vision "What is the structure of this scene in space?" Depth estimation, point clouds, spatial mapping. How self-driving cars understand distance, how AR anchors objects to physical space, how robots navigate rooms.

Session Challenge · +5 PointsGuess What the Model Sees

Below are two scenes. For each one, a CV model is analysing the image. Your job: guess which CV task the model is performing, and what it would output. Think about the five pillars — which one fits what the model needs to do here?

Scene 01 — The Street

The model has analysed this street scene and drawn coloured boxes around objects with labels and numbers. Which CV pillar is this? What do the numbers next to each label mean — and why do they matter?

✓ Detection. The model identified objects and drew bounding boxes around their exact positions — that's what separates Detection from Classification (which would just say "this is a street scene" with no locations). The numbers — 0.96, 0.94, 0.89 — are confidence scores. A score of 0.96 means the model is 96% confident it found a car at that location. This matters because in a real system, you set a threshold: anything below 0.5, say, gets ignored. Too low a threshold → too many false alarms. Too high → you miss real objects. Finding the right threshold for the right use case is itself a research problem.

Scene 02 — The Medical Scan

A CV model has analysed a medical scan. It has coloured different regions and flagged something in red. The doctor sees this overlay on their screen. What task is the model performing — and what is it actually doing to the image at the pixel level?

✓ Segmentation. The model went through every single pixel in the scan and assigned it to a region — organ A, organ B, background, or anomaly. This is pixel-level understanding. A bounding box would not work here — a doctor needs to know the exact boundary of a tumour, not just roughly where it is. The red dashed region is the model flagging an anomaly it was not trained to expect — a deviation from what healthy tissue should look like at that pixel distribution. This is why CV in medicine is genuinely useful: it can process thousands of scans at the same consistency level a human radiologist can only sustain for a few hours.

Bonus Question · +3 Points

In Scene 01, the model found a person with a confidence score of 0.89 — lower than the cars at 0.96 and 0.94. Give one reason why detecting a person might be harder for the model than detecting a car in a street scene.

Good possible answers include: cars have rigid, consistent shapes — a car looks roughly the same from any angle, but a person changes shape drastically (standing, crouching, arms raised, wearing a coat). Scale variation — a person further away occupies far fewer pixels than a nearby car, giving the model less information. Occlusion — people are often partially hidden behind objects. Clothing variation — the model has to generalise across countless different appearances. Any one of these is a valid research-quality answer. Share yours in the group chat.
