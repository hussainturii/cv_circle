Part 1 — Paper ReadingDeep Residual Learning for Image Recognition

He et al., 2015 — arXiv:1512.03385 — CVPR 2016 Best Paper

We are going to read this paper using the Reading Template from Session 1: Problem → Method → Key Result → Limitation → My Question. For each step, the most important lines from the actual paper are pulled and highlighted so you are reading the authors' own words — not a summary.

P
Problem — what were they trying to solve?

By 2015, researchers knew that deeper neural networks should perform better — more layers means the model can learn more complex features. But in practice, something strange was happening: adding more layers made networks perform worse, not better. And this wasn't overfitting. The training error itself was going up. Something fundamental was broken.

From the paper — Introduction

"When deeper networks are able to start converging, a degradation problem has been exposed: with the network depth increasing, accuracy gets saturated and then degrades rapidly. Unexpectedly, such degradation is not caused by overfitting, and adding more layers to a suitably deep model leads to higher training error."

The authors call this the degradation problem. The intuition behind why it happens: as gradients flow backwards through many layers during training, they get multiplied by small numbers over and over and eventually vanish — so the early layers stop learning at all. The network can't use its depth.

From the paper — Introduction

"Is learning better networks as easy as stacking more layers? An obstacle to answering this question was the notorious problem of vanishing/exploding gradients, which hamper convergence from the beginning."

The Degradation Problem — Visualised

Fig 1 from the paper — the 56-layer network performs worse than the 20-layer one on training data. This is the degradation problem.

M
Method — what did they do about it?

The idea He et al. proposed is elegant. Instead of asking each layer to learn the full desired mapping H(x), let it learn the residual — the difference between the input and the output, written as F(x) = H(x) − x. The original input x is then added back via a shortcut connection that skips one or more layers.

From the paper — Section 3.1

"Instead of hoping each few stacked layers directly fit a desired underlying mapping, we explicitly let these layers fit a residual mapping. Formally, denoting the desired underlying mapping as H(x), we let the stacked nonlinear layers fit another mapping of F(x) := H(x) − x. The original mapping is recast into F(x) + x."

Why does this help? If additional layers are not needed, the model can simply push F(x) toward zero — making those layers behave like identity functions. The network can now decide to ignore the layers it doesn't need, rather than being forced to use them.

From the paper — Section 3.1

"If the added layers can be constructed as identity mappings, a deeper model should have training error no greater than its shallower counterpart. The degradation problem suggests that the solvers might have difficulties in approximating identity mappings by multiple nonlinear layers."

The shortcut connection adds no extra parameters and no computational cost. It is literally just adding the input back to the output. Simple — but the impact was enormous.

The Residual Block — What It Actually Looks Like

Left: a plain block forces every layer to learn the full mapping H(x). Right: a residual block only needs to learn F(x) — the leftover. The orange dashed path is the skip connection — it costs nothing and changes everything.

R
Key Result — what did they prove?

The results were decisive. ResNet-152 — a 152-layer network — achieved a top-5 error of 3.57% on the ImageNet test set, winning the ILSVRC 2015 competition. For context, human-level performance on this task is estimated around 5–10%. The model outperformed humans.

From the paper — Section 4.2

"Our single-model result has a top-5 validation error of 4.49%. Our ensemble has a top-5 error of 3.57%. This result won the 1st place on the ILSVRC 2015 classification task."

More importantly for the research community: ResNet-1202 — yes, 1202 layers — could actually be trained and produce reasonable results. That was unimaginable before skip connections. The degradation problem was solved.

From the paper — Section 4.2

"We further explore n = 200 layers for which we employ a 1202-layer network... We argue that this result shows that the optimization difficulty is fundamentally different from the degradation problem we initially observed."

ResNet vs Everything Before It — ImageNet Top-5 Error

ResNet's 3.57% top-5 error beat estimated human performance. Every bar is shorter = better. The drop from GoogLeNet to ResNet is the skip connection doing its work.

L
Limitation — what did they not fully solve?

The authors themselves note that at 1202 layers the network begins to show signs of overfitting — meaning it memorises the training data rather than generalising to new images. The skip connections solve the training problem, but they don't automatically solve the generalisation problem at extreme depth.

From the paper — Section 4.2

"We argue that this result shows that the optimization difficulty is fundamentally different... there is still an open question of how to improve regularization when depths are extremely large."

There's also something the paper doesn't fully explain: why does learning the residual F(x) rather than H(x) make training easier? The skip connection works empirically — the results are undeniable — but the theoretical explanation came later in follow-up work. This is a pattern you'll see often in deep learning research: something works before it's fully understood.

Q
My Question — what would you investigate next?

This is where your reading template ends and your thinking begins. Here are the questions this paper raises — questions that real researchers went on to investigate after 2015:

If skip connections every 2 layers work, do skip connections every 1 layer work better? What about every 3? Is the shortcut architecture optimal, or just the first one that worked? — This led to DenseNet (2016), which connects every layer to every other layer.

The skip connection adds x directly. What if instead of adding, you could learn how much of x to pass through? — This question led to attention mechanisms and, eventually, Transformers.

Open Notebook — Session 2

Part A
Reproduce the Degradation Problem

You'll train two plain networks — a 20-layer and a 56-layer — on CIFAR-10. No skip connections. Watch the 56-layer network perform worse on training data than the 20-layer one. This is the exact result from Figure 1 of the paper happening live on your screen.

# Plain block — no skip connection class PlainBlock(nn.Module): def forward(self, x): out = self.conv1(x) out = self.bn1(out) out = F.relu(out) out = self.conv2(out) out = self.bn2(out) return F.relu(out) # just the output, no x added

Run both training loops and compare the final training accuracy. The 56-layer should be noticeably worse. If it's not, check your depth configuration — that's a debug exercise built into the notebook.

Part B
Add the Skip Connection — Fix It

Now add the skip connection to the same block. One line of code. Train again and watch what changes. This is the core experiment of the paper reproduced in about 10 minutes of training time.

# Residual block — skip connection added class ResidualBlock(nn.Module): def forward(self, x): identity = x # save input out = self.conv1(x) out = self.bn1(out) out = F.relu(out) out = self.conv2(out) out = self.bn2(out) out += identity # THIS is the skip connection — one line return F.relu(out)

The difference between PlainBlock and ResidualBlock is exactly one line: out += identity. That's the entire contribution of the ResNet paper, reduced to its essence.

Part C
Visualise the Gradient Flow

Train both networks and plot the gradient magnitudes at each layer during backpropagation. You will see the gradients shrinking toward zero in the plain network — this is the vanishing gradient problem made visible. In the residual network, gradients stay healthy even in the earliest layers.

This is the most important cell in the notebook. It turns an abstract concept into something you can literally see on a graph.

Part D — Experiments
Try These — Each One Teaches Something Different
Experiment 1

Remove the skip connection from the ResidualBlock and retrain the 56-layer network. How does the training curve change? How quickly does it get worse compared to the plain network?

Experiment 2

Change the skip connection from addition to concatenation: replace out += identity with out = torch.cat([out, identity], dim=1). What breaks? Why does addition work but concatenation causes a problem here?

Experiment 3

Add a skip connection that skips 3 layers instead of 2. Does it still work? Does it work better or worse? The paper uses 2-layer skips — is that the only option?

Experiment 4 — The Interesting One

Set all the weights in the second conv layer of a ResidualBlock to zero before training. What does the block output? Think about this before running — then verify. This is what "identity mapping" means in practice.

Question 01
What is the "degradation problem" that ResNet was designed to solve?

The degradation problem is when adding more layers makes the training error go up — not just test error. This rules out overfitting as the cause. It means the optimiser itself struggles to train very deep networks without skip connections.

What does a residual block learn, compared to a plain block?

Question 06
In the ResidualBlock code, what is the single line that implements the skip connection?

out += identity is the skip connection — it adds the saved input x back to the transformed output before the final ReLU. Everything else in the block also exists in a plain network. This one line is what makes it a residual block.

Question 07
In Experiment 4, if you set the second conv layer weights to zero before training, what does the block output?

If F(x) = 0, then output = F(x) + x = 0 + x = x. The block becomes a perfect identity function — it passes the input through unchanged. This is exactly what the paper means by "identity mapping" and it's why skip connections allow the network to safely have extra layers without hurting performance.

Code
Question 08
Why does replacing out += identity with torch.cat([out, identity], dim=1) break the block?

Concatenation joins tensors along a dimension — so if out has 64 channels and identity has 64 channels, the result has 128 channels. The next layer expects 64. The shapes don't match and PyTorch throws a size mismatch error. Addition keeps the dimensions identical, which is why it works cleanly.

Question 09
In the gradient visualisation cell, what do you expect to see in the plain 56-layer network compared to the ResNet?

This is the vanishing gradient problem made visible. In the plain network, gradients are multiplied through many layers and shrink toward zero — early layers receive almost no learning signal. In ResNet, the skip connection provides a direct gradient highway back to early layers, keeping the magnitudes large enough to train effectively.

Question 10
Experiment 3 adds a skip connection that skips 3 layers instead of 2. Based on what you know, which outcome is most likely?

Skip connections work for various lengths — 1, 2, 3 layers. The 2-layer version from the paper is well-studied but not the only option. DenseNet (2016) extended this idea by connecting every layer to every other layer. Whether 3-layer skips are better or worse than 2-layer is genuinely an empirical question that depends on the task, dataset, and depth. This is the kind of question good research asks.
